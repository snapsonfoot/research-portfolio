Case Study #1

Lapsed User Outreach: Understanding 6-8 Week Churn

🧠 The Problem

6-8 week users showed elevated churn risk, but cancellation codes didn't explain why. Reason codes like "Too Expensive" or "Too Much Product" varied little from other cohorts, masking the real friction points.

🎯 Research Questions

  • Are cancellation reason codes truly reflective of user motivations?
  • What sources of friction are we not capturing through quantitative data?
  • What changes would most reduce churn among 6–8 week users?

🛠 Methods

Semi-structured interviews (n=55) with recently canceled users (within 30 days), segmented by top three cancellation reasons: Too Expensive, Too Much Product, and Other. Paired with quantitative review of cancellation codes and comments.

📊 Key Findings

  • "Too Much Product" users: Automated shipments were piling up. While plans were flexible, controls weren't intuitive enough to adapt to changing consumption. Users cited competitors' simple "yes/no" shipment prompts.
  • "Too Expensive" users: Beyond price sensitivity, many were unwilling to pay for a service that sometimes sent products they didn't like. The "always something different" variety model created too much volatility.
  • "Other" users: A consistent theme emerged: requests for a loyalty program. Users ready to commit long-term found no reward structure for doing so.

🎬 Impact & Changes

  • Simplified shipment controls: Added "Quick Actions" to upcoming shipment emails (delay/expedite with one click). Drove 15% increase in email click-through rate.
  • Repeat favorites option: Users could re-order highly-rated products or switch to a subscription repeating their favorite. Subscriptions using this feature showed 20% higher retention than variety-based plans.
  • Prepay plans: While a traditional loyalty program wasn't viable, expanded prepay options gave users a benefit for committing longer-term, filling the same psychological role.

📝 Evolution of Thinking

In future research, I would cross-reference user-specific cancellation data with interview transcripts to identify patterns between stated reasons and actual behavior—helping validate which qualitative findings are most actionable at scale.

Case Study #2

Influencer Ad Read Impact: What Drives Conversion

🧠 The Problem

Marketing needed a clearer blueprint for influencer ad-reads. We wanted to identify specific messaging strategies and presentation formats that resonated most with prospective customers.

🎯 Research Questions

  • What are the components of a successful influencer ad-read?
  • What specific approaches to our product garner positive responses from viewers?

🛠 Methods

Short-form survey (n=400) to validate directional trends, paired with semi-structured interviews (n=40) for deeper context. Audience: New customers within 20-30 days of first purchase.

📊 Key Findings

  • Visual presentation drives engagement: Viewers responded most to tactile product demos. High production value helped but wasn't necessary if the influencer physically interacted with the product (e.g., preparing it on camera).
  • "Small business" framing builds trust: Audiences associated this with ethical sourcing and transparency, countering skepticism of D2C brands.
  • Personal experience > feature list: Anecdotes like "X was my favorite" drove stronger connection than feature recitations. Variety is best conveyed through story, not a list.

🎬 Impact & Changes

Updated influencer briefings to encourage:

  • Tactile product display (physically interact with product on camera)
  • Highlighting independent small-business sourcing
  • Focus on personal experience over feature lists

📝 Evolution of Thinking

In future work, I would pair qualitative feedback with ad performance data (click-through rates, conversion) to quantify the impact of different ad-read formats and validate which approaches drive the strongest business outcomes.

Case Study #3

High Intent Leads, Low Conversion: Onboarding Friction

🧠 The Problem

High-intent leads (those submitting emails but not converting) were converting at lower rates than prior months. We needed to understand specific friction points preventing sign-up completion to guide the next onboarding iteration.

🎯 Research Questions

  • What specific barriers were preventing high-intent users from converting?
  • What changes to messaging or UX could reduce decision friction and increase trust?

🛠 Methods

Survey (n=400) and interviews (n=35) with high-intent users who submitted emails but didn't convert, contacted within 2 weeks of initial outreach.

📊 Key Findings

  • Plan complexity creates pause: The tiered pricing model (two tiers, multiple plans) forced users to "do the math," leading many to abandon sign-up rather than risk choosing wrong.
  • Unclear post-promo pricing undermines trust: Users were unsure what would happen after the intro period: Would shipping still be free? How much would renewal cost?
  • Timing uncertainty leads to drop-off: Our product is a daily essential—users hesitated to switch without a clear delivery window, preferring to stick with their current supply rather than risk running out.

🎬 Impact & Changes

  • Simplified onboarding UI: Redesigned funnel to emphasize a single "recommended plan," reducing cognitive load and reassuring users they could adjust later.
  • Clearer renewal messaging: Updated promo language to frame offers as one-time intro deals, with transparent post-trial pricing (including shipping) shown upfront.
  • Estimated delivery date: Even though fulfillment times varied, providing an estimated arrival date gave users something concrete to anchor their decision.

📝 Evolution of Thinking

In future projects, I would incorporate quick usability testing around alternative plan/price displays—even just mockups—to see which approaches resonate before committing to a full redesign. This would de-risk the solution and validate assumptions earlier.
